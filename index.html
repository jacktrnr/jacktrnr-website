About

Jackson recently completed his PhD in Applied Mathematics at Columbia University, where he characterized a novel class of coherent wave excitations and their dynamical stability as manifestations of spontaneous symmetry breaking in nonlinear Schrödinger equations.

His broader research spans spectral and scattering theory, bifurcation analysis, numerical eigenvalue solvers, and spectral geometry. Previously, he developed stability analysis tools for plasmas in magnetic confinement fusion.

2025 · Preprint

Resonance-induced Nonlinear Bound States

J. Turner & M. I. Weinstein

We study how localized, time-periodic bound states of the nonlinear Schrödinger equation can bifurcate directly from the continuous spectrum at energies associated with scattering and transmission resonances of the underlying linear potential. Rather than requiring a pre-existing linear eigenvalue, these nonlinear bound states are induced by the resonance itself: the nonlinearity converts a long-lived scattering state into a genuinely localized excitation. We characterize the bifurcation, the resulting soliton profiles, and their dependence on the resonance structure — connecting classical scattering theory to the existence of coherent nonlinear waves.

2025 · In preparation

Linear Stability Analysis of Ideal Magnetohydrodynamics

J. Turner & D. Crews

We develop a linear stability framework for ideal magnetohydrodynamic flows in cylindrical geometry, targeting the shear-driven instabilities (Mack modes) relevant to magnetically confined plasmas. Perturbations are reduced to a generalized eigenvalue problem whose spectrum determines growth rates and mode structure; we solve it with adaptive, high-resolution eigenvalue solvers that resolve the sharp critical layers where these instabilities concentrate. The result is a tool for mapping the stability boundaries of confined-plasma configurations.

Manuscript in preparation

2023 · Journal

Flat Tori with Large Laplacian Eigenvalues

SIAM Journal on Applied Algebra and Geometry

This work studies the shape optimization problem of choosing a flat torus — a quotient of Euclidean space by a lattice — that maximizes its Laplace eigenvalues under a volume constraint. We analyze how the optimal lattice geometry changes with dimension, construct and certify optimizers in dimensions up to eight, and relate the extremal configurations to classical lattice-packing problems. The results give sharp, explicit examples in the geometry of the Laplacian spectrum.

2022 · Preprint

A Generalized Expansion Method for Computing Laplace–Beltrami Eigenfunctions

Numerical spectral geometry on manifolds

We present a spectral expansion method for computing eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of the Laplace–Beltrami operator on curved domains and manifolds. By expanding in a well-chosen global basis and reducing the eigenproblem to a structured matrix pencil, the method achieves high accuracy for smooth geometries while avoiding the mesh-generation overhead of finite-element approaches. We demonstrate convergence and accuracy across a range of model geometries.

2021 · Thesis

Expansion Method for Eigenvalue Problems

Honors Thesis · University of Utah

My undergraduate honors thesis introduces the expansion-based approach to Laplacian eigenvalue problems that later grew into the Laplace–Beltrami work above. It develops the method from first principles, establishes its accuracy on canonical domains, and lays out the numerical foundations for computing eigenfunctions of the Laplacian operator.
